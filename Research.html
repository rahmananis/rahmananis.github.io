Research

Defect-rich Dopnat-free Dilute Ferromagnetic Semiconducting Metal Oxides for Spin-electronic Applications

Modern solid-state electronics is often developed in parallel with or directly through the evolution of structures based on their “champion” materials. These conventional applications are based mainly on manipulating the electron charge. An intriguing alternative is the field of spin-based electronics, in which the manipulation of the electron spin in semiconductor devices promises higher speed, higher storage capacity, more efficiency, and lower power consumption in a wide range of applications including magnetic storage, magnetic memories, quantum computers, quantum communication, sensors, and other information and communications technology devices. Dilute ferromagnetic semiconductors belong to one such unique class of materials in which the semiconducting and ferromagnetic properties are combined in a single material.

However, the realization of functional spin-based devices for information processing remains elusive due to several fundamental challenges such as the low Curie temperature of group III–V and II–VI semiconductors (<200 K), and the low spin-injection efficiencies of existing III–V, II–VI, and transparent conductive oxide semiconductors in a multilayer device structure, which are caused by precipitation and migration of dopants from the host layer to the adjacent layers.

Clearly, there is an acute need for an alternative approach to the traditional method of incorporating dopants as a means to create the desirable properties. My project is based on defect-rich dopant-free transparent dilute ferromagnetic semiconductor oxides nanostructures, where the Ferromagnetic property could be controlled by controlling the different types of oxygen vacancy defects. For example, we establish that oxygen vacancy defects are primarily responsible for the origin of ferromagnetism in undoped ZrO2 nanostructures and their magnetic properties could be controlled by manipulating the composition of oxygen vacancy defects within the host lattice.

Novel transition metal oxide nanomaterials for ultra-efficient photoelectrochemical water splitting

Ultrasmall transition metal and metal oxide nanoclusters (less than 10 nm in dia.) are produced by a nanocluster generation source (Nanogen) based on gas-phase aggregation technique using DC magnetron sputtering. The precise size-selection is performed by using a high resolution quadrupole mass filter coupled with the Nanogen system. The shape, size and crystallinity of the nanoclusters are appropriately controlled by controlling various parameters such as chamber pressure, gas flow rate, deposition temperature, power supply, source to substrate distance, aggregation zone length etc.

Highly size-dependent photoelectrochemical water splitting properties are investigated using these supported nanoclusters (often combinatorially) on various substrates.

Nanoscale Characterization

Nanomaterials properties and the micro/nanodevice characteristics are analyzed using various characterization tools such as FESEM, TEM, AFM, XRD, XPS, Hall Measurement, Semiconductor Parameter Analyzer coupled with a probe station and electrochemical analyzer.
Currently, I am working on defect-rich hierarchical nanowires to achieve enhanced performance for sensing, hydrogen production from water, and spin-electronic applications. In another project, I am trying to investigate the effect of plasmonic metallic nanoclusters in the photoelectrochemical water splitting reaction based on transition metal oxide on hierarchical nanowires.
